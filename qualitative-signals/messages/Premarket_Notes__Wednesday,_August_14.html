July's CPI was reported inline (2.0%). The year/year CPI was slightly better than expected at 2.9% versus 3.0%. Markets look suspicious of the third consecutive month of an exactly 0.10% beat of expectations. May we suggest you wear your seatbelts and prepare for a bumpy takeoff.

 

Yesterday, traders were back to the multiple-rate-cut view they held at the start of the year, but this time around, concerns about an economic slowdown (recession) seem to outweigh inflation worries. Our concern is that multiple rate cuts could reignite inflation again.

 

Market Sentiment Indicators (Investopedia): We thought this was worth a quick read.

 

VIX: The VIX reflects market volatility derived from options markets. A VIX above 15 implies a shift from bullish conditions to a slight pullback, while readings above 20 suggest a 5-10% corrective phase. The recent climb above 60 could signal a more substantial and prolonged market decline, indicating that investors should brace for potential turbulence.

 

AAII Survey Dynamics: The AAII survey gauges individual investor sentiment. About four weeks ago, bullish sentiment spiked above 50%, serving as a red flag for potential corrections. Bullishness has dropped from 52% to 40%, while bearish sentiment has risen from 22% to 38%, suggesting a growing pessimism. This shift indicates investors are becoming more cautious, hinting at a potentially tradable low.

 

NAAIM Exposure Trends: The NAAIM Exposure Index tracks sentiment among professional money managers. A bullish reading above 50% signals euphoria. Readings above 100% indicate excessive leverage and optimism among money managers. Five weeks ago, this index was just over 100%, suggesting extreme bullishness. Since early July, however, there's been a noticeable shift, with money managers reducing their equity exposure from leveraged long positions to more neutral or slightly underweight stances. This pivot toward a defensive posture suggests professionals are preparing for further declines.

 

The three sentiment indicators suggest potential market weakness ahead. Elevated VIX readings, shifting sentiment among individual investors, and money managers lightening their equity positions all indicate a possible extended pullback. While these indicators provide valuable insights, watching for signs of accumulation and a break above key levels is crucial to confirm any market recovery.

 

Having said all that, the S&P 500 has recovered some of its recent losses and is now less than 5% from its record high set in July.
