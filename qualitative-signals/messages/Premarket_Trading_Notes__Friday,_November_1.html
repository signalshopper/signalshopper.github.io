Markets are pulling back from earlier gains after nonfarm payrolls missed expectations (12 K versus 113 K expected), but we note the number was likely affected by hurricanes and strikes last month. However, declining Treasury yields and a weaker USD suggest risk assets will rise today.

 

Will higher, not lower, rates come to a country near you? Yesterday, UK 10YR gov’t bond yields rose to 4.43% after the new Labour Government delivered its budget and “revealed that gilt issuance would increase by £19.2 billion to £296.9 billion this fiscal year, and rise by more than analysts had expected in the years to come.” Yep, you guessed it, “the only way to drive economic growth in the UK is to invest, invest, invest.” If you recall the $1.9T Inflation Reduction Act, you understand that investing is code for spending. The UK Chancellor explained the need to increase taxes by £40 billion. Twenty-five billion will come from employer (employee) national health insurance contributions, carried interest taxes will rise to 32% from 28%, and the surcharge on second homes will increase from 3% to 5%. In short, larger budgets require more borrowing (bond issuance) and higher rates.
