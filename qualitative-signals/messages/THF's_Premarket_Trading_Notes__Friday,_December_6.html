Markets look to open higher after a better-than-expected employment rebound from last month’s Boeing and hurricane-affected results. However, enthusiasm seems slightly tempered by a rising unemployment rate (4.2% versus 4.1% last month). In other words, markets should continue to expect a December rate reduction. We believe the market will open higher and then move lower.     

 

Will markets continue to rise? Market tops are often found when bearish prognosticators throw in the towel. Yesterday, perma-bear David Rosenberg said: “The remarkable surge in the S&P 500 over the past two years has truly surpassed my expectations, especially in the last twelve months. Given that this bull market has persisted long enough, those of us who have found ourselves on the wrong side of the trade must consider adopting.”

 

Adding to the growing fear of a market top is well-known Economist Ed Yardeni, a bull, who notes, “The S&P 500 is now 11.2% above its 200-day moving average, signaling that the market may be overbought, with financials especially overextended.” Yardeni also notes that “the number of bullish views is at a historical high of 62.9%, and the consumer confidence index survey for November showed that 56.4% of consumers expect stocks to be higher in 12 months, a record that is even more bullish than just before the dot-com bubble burst in 2000.” We think everyone should calm down and stay in the boat. Excluding the tech bubble of 2000 and the 2008 mortgage meltdown, markets have risen in seven out of the last nine election years.  

 

Pitchforks at the Healthcare Gates: The motive behind the killing of UNH’s CEO, Brian Thompson, has yet to be confirmed, but the words found on shell casings at the scene of his shooting suggest seething public discontent with health insurers. According to police, bullets were marked with the words “deny,” defend,” and “depose,” and are believed to refer to a 2010 book, “Delay, Deny, Defend: Why Insurance Companies Don’t Pay Claims and What You Can Do About It,” which focused on insurance companies’ efforts to avoid paying health insurance claims. We note that denials accelerated as premiums rose after implementing the Affordable Care Act. “Experian Health recently released a report showing that three out of every four healthcare providers had experienced a rise in the number of claims denied by payers between 2022 and 2024.”

 

The ultimate fallout from this heinous crime is unknown, but change is coming. Yesterday, UnitedHealth Group (UNH) dropped 5.2%, Cigna (CI) fell 2.3%, Aetna-parent CVS Health (CVS) lost 2.1%, and Humana (HUM) slid 1.7%.
