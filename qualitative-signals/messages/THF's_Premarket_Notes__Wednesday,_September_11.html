Inflation: “The annual inflation rate in the US slowed for a fifth consecutive month to 2.5% in August (versus 2.6% expected), the lowest since February 2021, and down from 2.9% in July. Compared to July, the CPI rose 0.2%, the same as in July, matching expectations. The index for shelter rose 0.5% and was the main factor in the monthly increase. Core inflation steadied at a three-year low of 3.2%, but the monthly core inflation rate edged up to 0.3% from 0.2%, which was above forecasted at 0.2%.” ( US Bureau of Labor Statistics)

 

Opinion: If inflation is indeed receding - despite the 0.5% rise in rent inflation, a preferred measure of inflation - the Federal Reserve need not be too aggressive when reducing the Fed Funds rate (currently 5.25% - 5.50%). However, “core CPI, excluding food and energy, rose 0.3% in August, above forecasts of 0.2% and up from 0.2% in July, with a year-on-year gain of 3.2%, signaling persistent inflation pressures.”  

 

Banks: After talking about bank headwinds ad nauseam, we finally received a whiff of capitulation yesterday after JP Morgan (JPM) and Goldman Sachs (GS) informed attendees at Barclay’s financial services conference that net interest margins (the spread on money lent to borrowers) and trading income would be lower than expected. The icing on the cake was lender Ally Financial (ALLY), who reported that consumer credit trends are a problem as auto loan delinquencies rose 20% more than expected, while debt charge-offs were running 10% above expectations. Not to be outdone, the Federal Reserve waved a white flag yesterday, announcing it reduced the anticipated 19% rise in capital requirements for the largest US banks - meant to protect America’s banking system – to 9%. Is the Federal Reserve concerned bank balance sheets can’t support the higher capital requirements? We remain cautious on the banks.
