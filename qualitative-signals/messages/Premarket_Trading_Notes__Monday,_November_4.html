Why are Treasury yields rising if Fed fund rates are declining? Treasury yields have risen +0.75% since September’s 50 bps rate reduction. Why? Because we are $36 trillion in debt, and the bipartisan appetite for inflationary deficits will lead to more money printing. The bond market knows that the Treasury is kicking the can down the road and will soon have to sell a ton of 10 to 30-year treasuries on the market. The bond market knows the Treasury has no choice, so it’s demanding higher premiums as a result.

 

Why the Fed will cut rates: Friday’s jobs numbers, impacted by the hurricanes and Boeing strike, are less meaningful than the JOLTs numbers (job openings and quits), as both continued to fall. In addition, the weaker-than-expected 3Q GDP print (2.8% Y/Y) and the continued contraction of the ISM Manufacturing numbers suggest the Fed has no choice but to reduce the Fed Funds rate by another 25 bps.

 

Why Treasury yields will remain high: The problem is the US has $36 trillion of debt on the books, and the intuitive bond market knew the Fed was oiling the printing presses for another $1.4 trillion dollar-palooza. The Treasury confirmed as much when it announced its quarterly refunding plans for the 4th quarter: Spend $186 billion from the TGA (Treasury checking account), issue $546 billion more USTs, and, for the 1Q of 2025, fill the TGA back up to $850 billion (by adding $150 billion back) and borrowing an additional $823 billion. Of course, when the printing presses are rolling, bitcoin, stocks, commodities, and gold tend to rise, but so do constrictive consumer lending rates (credit card interest, mortgage rates, etc.).

 

We could add a layer of complexity and talk about declining liquidity in the repo market and the end of quantitative tightening (QT). However, all we need to know is that the [inflationary] Bank Term Lending Program (repo market) is likely to be revived by necessity. Why? Inflation is accelerating while economic growth is decelerating (stagflation?).
